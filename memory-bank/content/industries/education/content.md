## Voice & Tone Guidelines

### Brand Voice
- **Supportive education partner:** Not a vendor pushing products, but a knowledgeable partner who understands education challenges
- **Transparent and honest:** Directly address pain points and provide clear information
- **Technically competent:** Demonstrate deep understanding of education IT requirements
- **Respectful of decision-making:** Recognize that education purchasing involves committees and long cycles

### Tone Variations by Context

**Hero/Benefits Sections:**
- Confident and reassuring
- Focus on outcomes and peace of mind
- Example: "Protect student data, ensure learning continuity, and plan your IT budget with confidence"

**Challenges Sections:**
- Empathetic and understanding
- Acknowledge real pain without dramatizing
- Example: "You need a reliable partner who understands education workflows"

**Solutions/Features:**
- Clear, specific, benefit-focused
- Lead with the benefit, support with technical details
- Example: "Auto-scaling handles exam period traffic spikes. Transparent pricing means no surprise bills"

**FAQ:**
- Detailed and helpful
- Remove uncertainty and objections
- Technical but accessible
- Example: "Our CloudEdge infrastructure is LMS-agnostic - if it runs on Linux/Windows servers..."

**CTAs:**
- Action-oriented but not pushy
- Respect the evaluation process
- Example: "Schedule education consultation" not "Buy now"

### Writing Rules

1. **Always use active voice:** "Protect student data" not "Student data is protected"
2. **Sentence case for headings/buttons:** "View transparent pricing" not "View Transparent Pricing"
3. **Short paragraphs:** Maximum 2-3 sentences
4. **Specific metrics:** Use real numbers (99.9%, 15 minutes, 40% reduction)
5. **Address as "you/your":** Speak directly to IT decision-makers
6. **Lead with benefits:** What outcome they get, then how it works
7. **No jargon:** If technical terms are needed, explain them
8. **Concise CTAs:** Maximum 3 words ideal, 4-5 acceptable

## Bilingual Considerations

### Arabic Translation Principles

1. **Meaning over literal translation:** Natural, professional Arabic that conveys the intent
2. **Keep established technical terms:** LMS, FERPA, SLA, CDN, SOC, EdTech remain in English
3. **Text expansion:** Arabic typically runs 25-30% longer - design must accommodate
4. **Formality level:** Professional but accessible (avoid overly classical or overly casual)
5. **Test with native speakers:** When possible, validate with Egyptian Arabic speakers

### RTL Layout Considerations

- Button labels may need shortening - pair with icons when possible
- Navigation menus reverse order in RTL
- Icons and graphics may need mirroring (arrows, directional elements)
- Numbers: Use Western numerals (123) for technical metrics, follow platform conventions
- Maintain left-to-right for: URLs, email addresses, product names (CloudEdge, Posta)

### Cultural Adaptations

- Education is highly valued - emphasize quality and reputation protection
- Budget constraints are real - transparency and predictability are major differentiators
- Family and community trust matter - emphasize privacy and security
- Government/ministry compliance important - mention Egyptian requirements explicitly
- Academic hierarchy respected - use formal terms like "أعضاء هيئة التدريس" (faculty) not casual terms
